# Module Descriptor

## General Objectives:

<p align="justify">

This module aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of advanced algorithms and data structures, covering fundamental concepts to advanced topics essential for competitive programmers and software engineers. Through a structured approach, it aims to familiarise students with the theoretical foundations, practical methodologies, and techniques in algorithmic problem-solving and optimization. By delving into advanced data structures, graph algorithms, string algorithms, dynamic programming, amortised analysis, computational geometry, and advanced competitive programming topics, students will gain a holistic view of modern algorithm design and implementation strategies.

</p>

## Learning outcomes:

On completion of the module, students will be able to:

1. Explain key concepts in advanced data structures, including linear data structures, universal hashing, and order statistics trees.
2. Solve using the principles of graph algorithms, including the Bellman-Ford-Moore algorithm and distance-vector protocols.
3. Implement string matching algorithms such as Knuth-Morris-Pratt (KMP) and Rabin-Karp to solve practical text processing problems.
4. Implement various dynamic programming techniques, including bitmasking DP, tree DP, and digit DP, to optimise algorithmic solutions.
5. Utilise advanced dynamic programming concepts, such as DP with pruning and DP speedup using matrix exponentiation, to optimise solutions for complex problems.
6. Assess the efficiency of different amortised analysis methods, including aggregate analysis, the accounting method, and the potential method.
7. Design advanced data structure solutions using techniques like heavy-light decomposition and centroid decomposition for complex algorithmic problems.
8. Investigate computational geometry concepts, including convex hull algorithms and line sweep techniques, to solve geometric problems efficiently.
9. Critique the effectiveness of different network flow algorithms and bipartite matching techniques in various practical scenarios.
10. Develop strategies for approaching NP-complete and NP-hard problems using problem reduction techniques and understanding of computational complexity.

## Assessment Approach:

Assessment components consist of Continuous Assessment (CA) Theory - 60% and Continuous Assessment (CA) Practical - 40%. The CA Theory will consist of Practical Work & Reports (30%) & Architectural Case Studies (30%) and CA Practical consists of a Final Project (40%). Assessments will be carried out continuously through the following assessment components:

**A. Practical Work & Reports: (20%)** Students are required to attend 2 hours of allocated weekly practical classes.

**B. Architectural Case Studies: (20%)** Students will undertake 2 architectural case studies as a group throughout the semester. Students will analyze and evaluate real-world software architectures, identifying strengths, weaknesses, and potential improvements

**C. Mid-Term Test: (20%)** During the 12th week, students will take an in-class,
closed-book quiz. The tutor will organise the quiz, which will cover 70%
of the syllabus content.

**D. Final Project: (40%)** Students will design and document a comprehensive software architecture for a complex system, incorporating all major concepts learned in the module. Students will undertake a final project as a group. The students will start selecting their project topic in the 7th week which they will have to cover all concepts learnt as they progress through the semester in the lectures and practical sessions. The project presentation will be done in the 14th and 15th weeks.

Pre-requisites: None
